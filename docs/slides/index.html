Good morning. This work asks whether a small fleet of drones can search an earthquake zone without a central brain telling each one where to go — and, just as importantly, whether we can prove one search strategy beats another. Two contributions: a decentralised, stigmergic searcher, and a seeded, statistical evaluation harness. I'll keep the talk to the claims the thesis can defend.

3 days.

After an earthquake, a survivor's odds collapse
once the golden period closes.

The clock is the whole motivation. Survival probability for someone trapped under rubble falls steeply over the first three days. Every hour spent not searching a region is paid for in lives, so the metric that matters is how fast and how completely you can cover ground. That reframes the engineering problem as a coverage-speed problem — the lens for the rest of the talk.

How the swarm searches

Steering: a stigmergic motor schema

Each drone sums five basis behaviours into one velocity vector — no drone is told where to go.
  • avoid pheromone others laid (spread out)
  • follow the assigned coverage pattern
  • avoid obstacles and peers
  • an anti-stall scatter phase
This is Arkin's motor schema. Each drone evaluates five basis behaviours every tick — avoid-visited, explore-unvisited, avoid-peers, stay-inside, goal-seek — emits a vector from each, and flies the weighted, normalised sum. Two of them, avoid-visited and explore-unvisited, read and write the shared pheromone field — which is exactly where stigmergy enters: no drone is ever told where to go, yet a saturated region repels the others because it is bright in the field they all read. There's also a scatter phase at startup and a von-Mises anti-stall fallback when the vectors cancel.

Stigmergy, live: coverage with no planner

A four-drone fleet sweeps a search disk under the real motor-schema law. Each lays a fading pheromone stamp under its sensor footprint; the field decays every tick. Drones are repelled by bright (visited) cells, pulled to dark ones, and held inside the disk — so the area fills with no planner, and victims are found as it does.

This is the thesis claim made visible. A four-drone fleet — the default fleet size — runs the full five-behaviour motor-schema locally: nothing plans a global path. After a brief scatter phase fans them into separate sectors, the two field behaviours take over: each drone deposits a Gaussian pheromone stamp under its sensor footprint, the field decays every tick, and drones are repelled by bright visited cells and pulled toward the dark unvisited ones. Stay-inside keeps them in the search disk; goal-seek nudges a drone toward a nearby victim. Watch coverage climb on the bar and the victims get found purely as a by-product of sweeping — coverage becomes detection. The honest cost the thesis names: this buys resilience but gives up a coverage guarantee, so coverage quality is something we measure, not prove — which is exactly what the evaluation does. Coverage: seven sweep patterns, one factory

The deliberative layer turns a chosen pattern into scan waypoints; the reactive layer flies them while staying spread out.

Pure reactivity wanders, so we overlay structure. A coverage strategy — spiral, expanding square, parallel track, and others — is a function from region, fleet, sensor width and seed to per-drone waypoints. It's a swappable strategy object: the deliberative layer lays the pattern down, the reactive motor-schema flies it while handling collisions and victim-seeking no static plan can. This separation is what lets the next chapter treat the pattern as the single independent variable.

Seeing: detection that earns its confidence

Raw sightings are noisy — a candidate is reported only after three guards in series:

① DBSCAN

clusters nearby detections; sparse noise points are dropped.

② Noisy-OR fusion

each independent witness raises the cluster's confidence.

③ Multi-witness gate

≥ k drones & P ≥ τ — blocks single-drone false alarms.
A single noisy sighting is not a victim. The detection filter accumulates sightings over a window, clusters them by position with DBSCAN, fuses each cluster's confidences with a noisy-OR rule so independent witnesses raise confidence, and only emits a candidate when both corroboration and a confidence threshold are met. That multi-witness gate is what stops one drone's false alarm from becoming a confirmed victim — and the confirmation threshold is the knob the operator tunes.

Dividing the work: a greedy auction

When a victim is confirmed, every eligible drone bids:
u = π · κdmax(dist, 1)

Homogeneous fleet → κd = 1, so highest bid = nearest eligible drone. A seeded, name-sorted tie-break makes ties reproducible.

A confirmed victim is auctioned. Each drone that is eligible — battery healthy, not failed, not already committed to a victim, not landing or returning to base — bids its utility, priority over distance, capability-scaled. The homogeneous fleet sets that capability weight to one, so the highest bid is just the nearest available drone. Watch D5: it's the closest, but its battery is low, so it never bids — that eligibility filter is what stops us dispatching a drone that can't finish the rescue, or double-assigning one. The seeded tie-break is what makes the winner independent of network message order, so the same seed allocates identically. The greedy auction is O(N) and provably optimal for a single task; round-robin and Hungarian are drop-in alternatives.

How it is built

Three tiers: deliberate, sequence, react

Slow global planning on top, fast reflexes at the bottom — the 3T architecture, each layer at its own clock rate.

The classic three-tier hybrid. Deliberation on top — the slow planner that runs the coverage strategy and the auction, ticking once a second. The reactive layer at the bottom — each drone's PID controller closing its loop fifty times a second, because flight is unforgiving. The executive in between, sequencing and supervising at five hertz. The key insight made temporal: the layers are not phases of one cycle, they are concurrent loops at rates separated by almost two orders of magnitude — the planner can afford to think because nothing waits on it, the controller can react because nothing blocks it.

Ports and adapters: the domain knows no ROS

The architecture is a strict dependency inversion. Build it up: at the centre, a pure-Python domain core — the Mission aggregate, the auction, the motor-schema, the clustering — that imports no rclpy and knows nothing of ROS or DDS. Around it, typed Protocol ports: MissionPort, StigmergyPort, and notably the three 3T-layer boundaries — DeliberativePlanner, ExecutiveSupervisor, BehaviouralLayer — so each tier depends on the next only through a typed contract. Only at the edge sit the adapters: driver adapters translate incoming ROS and Gazebo messages into port calls, driven adapters turn the core's outputs back into ROS/DDS traffic — and they are the only code that touches rclpy. The payoff: substitute fakes for the adapters and the auction, the patterns, the clustering are all unit-tested with no simulator running.

A deliberately chosen stack

Every layer picked against alternatives — ROS 2 over ROS 1, data-centric DDS over a broker, a Python/C++ split across the GIL boundary.

I'll be brief here — the stack is deliberate, not defaulted. ROS 2 over ROS 1 for the real-time, multi-process story; data-centric DDS with the QoS profiles that let a slow subscriber fall behind a best-effort sensor stream without ever stalling the fast publisher; and a Python core for the algorithms with C++ where the GIL would bite. Each choice is defended against its alternative in the implementation chapter.

The hybrid shape — two components, plus the hard part

Hybrid control's defining structure: deliberation up top, reaction at the bottom, and an intermediate coordinator reconciling them. The flow is asymmetric — deliberation informs; reaction overrides on surprise.

The defining structure of a hybrid: deliberation informs, reaction overrides. The coverage plan flows down as intent, but a sudden obstacle or a peer too close lets the reactive layer override it immediately — the plan is advice, not a command. That asymmetry is what makes the system both purposeful and safe.

Hybrid, not a pure swarm — a defended trade-off

The reactive core is behaviour-based; coverage and allocation are deliberative. Why not a pure swarm?

A pure swarm wins on

  • faithfulness to full decentralisation
  • robustness to disaster dynamics

But the hybrid keeps

  • a coverage guarantee (IAMSAR doctrine: "this region was searched")
  • reproducibility — the whole second contribution

Honest scope: steering is fully decentralised; one thin coordinator runs coverage + the auction. Pushing it peer-to-peer is future work, not a present claim.

This is the design decision I most expect to be challenged, so I'll defend it head-on. A pure swarm would be more faithful to full decentralisation and more robust to disaster dynamics. But the hybrid keeps two things a pure swarm gives up: a coverage guarantee — IAMSAR doctrine needs to assert "this region was searched" — and reproducibility, which is the entire second contribution. The honest scope, stated plainly: steering is fully decentralised; one thin coordinator still runs coverage and the auction. Making that peer-to-peer is future work, not a claim I'm making today.

And the seam is real — pluggable behaviour-based control

The reactive core opens at two strategy seams:

  • each basis behaviour is a first-class object in a registry — adding one = a class + register()
  • the combination rule is a swappable ArbitrationStrategy — subsumption ships beside motor-schema

So "plug in behaviour-based control" is structural, not rhetorical: a new controller enters by injection, with zero edits to the deliberative or executive layers.

The MotorSchemaArbitration default is bit-identical; SubsumptionArbitration ships beside it.

"Pluggable behaviour-based control" has to be structural, not a slogan. It is: each basis behaviour is a first-class object in a registry — adding one is a class plus a register call — and the way behaviours combine is itself a swappable ArbitrationStrategy, so subsumption ships right beside the motor-schema. A new reactive controller enters by injection with zero edits to the deliberative or executive layers. The MotorSchemaArbitration default reproduces the original weighted vector sum to the last bit, and a working SubsumptionArbitration demonstrator ships beside it — proof the seam is real, not rhetorical.

Does it work?

Turning a demo into evidence

  • One independent variable: the coverage pattern.
  • Baseline to beat: random_walk.
  • Every run seeded — same seed, same trajectory.
  • Each cell repeated; results with bootstrap CIs.

The statistics

Jain fairness index
Kaplan–Meier survival
Welch's t-test
Efron bootstrap intervals
The second contribution comes due here. The experiment has one independent variable — the coverage pattern — controlled scenario and seed, and SAR metrics as dependent variables. The baseline that licenses every claim is the random walk: "better" means better than undirected motion, full stop. Four statistical tools: the Jain index for fairness, Kaplan-Meier for how fast victims are found, Welch's t-test for significance because we can't assume equal variance, and the Efron bootstrap for confidence intervals because small trial counts make a normal approximation unsafe.

This is the slide I'd most want a committee to remember, because it's the only number in the talk that is measured rather than illustrative — and I report it precisely because of what it cost. For most of the project every recorded mission scored zero true positives: the headline claim, falsified by its own telemetry. Two bugs in series — a detection projection that ignored the drone's yaw, and a scorer that read the fusion gate instead of the saga's own confirmation events, exactly the distinction the formal model had named. Fix both and you get this: first detection at 20.5 seconds, first confirmation at 56, and victim 3 localised to within fourteen centimetres — one true positive, scored honestly against ground truth. Precision and recall are modest, and I'll defend that: recall is bounded by a 31% coverage timeout, precision by near-pad false positives — a perception problem, not a coordination one. The point is narrow and real: the system can find and confirm an actual victim, and the real-time factor of 0.37 is now measured, not estimated.

Briefly, the process: the work ran from January to May in overlapping stages — domain and architecture first, then the reactive and coordination layers, then the evaluation harness, and finally the documentation and the measured run. Honest boundaries, and where it goes next

Limitations

  • Reproducibility is statistical, not bit-exact (DDS ordering).
  • Perception is simulated, not real computer vision.
  • Single-host; the Hungarian allocator is under-exercised.

Future work

  • Sim-to-real: a real CV adapter behind the same port.
  • A full seeded sweep across all scenarios.
  • Predictive reassignment; a coverage-guarantee hybrid.
The boundaries, stated before anyone asks. Reproducibility is statistical, not bit-exact, because the physics engine isn't deterministic across cores — which is exactly why every result carries a confidence interval. Perception is simulated, not real computer vision, so the precision and recall measure the coordination and fusion pipeline, not a CV system. And it's one simulator on one host. The future-work crossings are the obvious ones: a real CV adapter behind the same port, a full archived sweep across scenarios, and pushing the coordinator peer-to-peer.

To close the argument: the contribution isn't a demo, it's a method held to a standard. A searcher whose drones steer themselves through stigmergy, and an evaluation harness that can say, with statistics rather than intuition, when one search beats another. The decentralisation is real at the steering layer and scoped honestly at the coordination layer — and that honesty is the point.

Backup for reproducibility questions: one root seed fans out into independent, offset-derived streams — environment, sensors, auction — so each subsystem is individually reproducible yet statistically uncorrelated with the others. This is what makes "same seed, same trajectory" hold at the strategy level despite the physics engine's cross-core nondeterminism.

Backup: the mission lifecycle

Backup for the lifecycle: a readiness handshake gates the start — the mission only advances from deploying to scanning once every drone has reported odometry steadily for a minimum duration, latched over transient-local QoS so a late subscriber still sees it. And the saga's recovery: if a drone fails mid-rescue, the victim it held is rolled back to detected and re-auctioned, and its sector is absorbed by a neighbour — a single failure rewinds only that drone's work.
